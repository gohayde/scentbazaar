```
[STEP 1: CHOOSE YOUR FRAGRANCE OIL - 0:30-1:00]
First, start with a fragrance oil. We carry 2,000+ oils at Scent Bazaar—from classic

Choose an oil you're passionate about. You'll be smelling this every day, so pick

[STEP 2: GET YOUR SUPPLIES - 1:00-1:45]
You'll need:
- Perfumers alcohol (95%+ ethanol) — this is the solvent that holds your fragrance
- Glass bottles (amber preferred to protect from UV light)
- A measuring scale (accurate to 1 gram)
- Pipettes or droppers for precision
- Labels (for your final product)

Pro tip: All of these are available here at Scent Bazaar, or I'll link them in the
description.

[STEP 3: MIX YOUR RATIO - 1:45-3:00]
Now for the chemistry. Professional perfume has a specific concentration of fragrance
oil to alcohol:
- Eau de Parfum (EDP): 15-20% fragrance oil, 80-85% alcohol
- Eau de Toilette (EDT): 5-15% fragrance oil, 85-95% alcohol
- Cologne: 2-5% fragrance oil

For beginners, I recommend starting with EDT—it's forgiving and gives good scent
projection without being overwhelming.

Let me show you the math. If I'm making 100ml of EDT with 15% concentration:
- 100ml × 0.15 = 15ml fragrance oil
- 100ml - 15ml = 85ml perfumers alcohol

Measure carefully. Use a scale for the most accurate results.

[STEP 4: BLEND AND AGE - 3:00-3:45]
Pour your fragrance oil into a glass bottle first, then add the alcohol slowly. Stir
gently—don't shake, which can create bubbles that dull the scent.

Here's the secret that most people miss: your perfume needs to age. For 24-48 hours,
the fragrance will settle and the scent will develop. Store it in a cool, dark place
during this time.

After aging, you'll notice the scent is richer and more complex. That's the fragrance
notes opening up.

[STEP 5: BOTTLE AND TEST - 3:45-4:45]
Once it's aged, pour your perfume into final spray bottles. Label it with:
- The fragrance name
- The concentration (EDP, EDT, etc.)
- The date you made it

Test it on your wrist. Apply a small amount (not too much—a little goes a long way).
The scent should last 4-6 hours on your skin.

If it's too strong, add a bit more alcohol. If it's too weak, add more fragrance oil
and re-age for another 24 hours.

[OUTRO - 4:45-5:00]
That's it! You've made your first perfume. If you want to start a brand or scale up,
we at Scent Bazaar can help with private label services, bottling, and distribution.
```

```
[INTRO - 0:00-0:20]
One of the most common questions I get: "Can I just use vodka to make perfume?"

Short answer: No. And here's why.

[WHY VODKA DOESN'T WORK - 0:20-1:30]
Vodka is typically 40% alcohol by volume. For perfume, you need 95% pure ethanol,
minimum.

Here's what happens with vodka:
- The water content (60%) dilutes your fragrance unevenly
- It can cause your fragrance oils to separate
- The scent won't project properly on skin
- Your perfume will have a short shelf life (weeks, not months)
- It can smell "off" because of vodka's flavor compounds

Think of it like this: you're making a precise chemical solution. If you use 60%
the wrong ingredient, your entire formula breaks down.

[WHAT IS PERFUMERS ALCOHOL? - 1:30-2:45]
Perfumers alcohol (also called cosmetic-grade alcohol or ethanol) is specifically
formulated for fragrance. Here's what makes it different:

1. Purity: 95%+ ethanol, tested for consistency
2. Additives: It contains denaturants (to prevent drinking) and sometimes bittering
agents
3. UV protection: Some premium grades include UV stabilizers to protect fragrance
from sun damage
4. Evaporation rate: Designed to evaporate at the right speed so your scent develops
gradually on skin

We use denatured perfumers alcohol because it's safe to use on skin and meets IFRA
(International Fragrance Association) standards.

[REAL WORLD COMPARISON - 2:45-3:45]
Let me show you the difference. I have two samples:

[SHOW VODKA vs PERFUMERS ALCOHOL]

When I add fragrance oil to vodka, notice how it separates? The oil floats because
of the high water content.

When I add the same oil to perfumers alcohol, it dissolves completely and uniformly.
That's what you need for a professional perfume.

Over time (let's fast-forward), the vodka-based sample has turned cloudy and the
scent has degraded. The perfumers alcohol sample is crystal clear and still smells
strong.

[CONCLUSION - 3:45-4:00]
Use quality perfumers alcohol. It's not that much more expensive, and it's the
difference between a hobby project and a product you'd actually use.
```

```
sprayed on a Chanel or Dior, you've smelled French perfume.

But what's actually the difference? And which should you use in your fragrance?

Let's break it down.

[SECTION 1: WHAT IS OUD? - 0:30-1:45]
Oud (also spelled agarwood) comes from the Aquilaria tree, native to Southeast Asia
and parts of the Middle East. When the tree is infected with a specific fungus, it
produces a resin to protect itself. That resin is oud, and it's incredibly rare and
expensive.

[HOLD UP OUD SAMPLE]

Oud smells deep, woody, slightly spicy, and earthy. It's often described as ancient
or mysterious. A single drop can scent an entire room.

The catch? Pure oud oil can cost $500-$5,000 per kilogram because it's so rare.
Most commercial oud fragrances use a synthetic version that costs about $50-100/kg
but captures the essence of the real thing.

In the Middle East, oud has been prized for centuries. It's mentioned in the Quran
and has deep cultural significance. When you wear oud, you're wearing tradition.

[SECTION 2: WHAT IS FRENCH PERFUME? - 1:45-3:15]
French perfume is a specific style of formulation that developed in Grasse, France,
starting in the 1600s.

[HOLD UP FRENCH FRAGRANCE SAMPLES]

French perfumes are typically made from:
- Floral notes (rose, jasmine, lilies)
- Citrus notes (bergamot, lemon, orange)
- Subtle spices (sometimes)
- Musk and vanilla undertones

The style is elegant, subtle, and sophisticated. A French perfume is designed to
develop over time—you spray it once, and over the next 2-4 hours, the fragrance
slowly unfolds with different notes becoming prominent.

For example, Chanel No. 5 opens with citrus (very fresh), develops floral notes in
the middle, and closes with a musk and sandalwood base that lasts 8+ hours.

French perfume is lighter and more refined than oud. It's designed for precision
and subtlety.

[SECTION 3: OUD VS. FRENCH - SIDE-BY-SIDE - 3:15-4:30]
Let me compare them directly:

**Strength:**
- Oud: Very strong, projects 10+ feet, one drop is potent
- French: Subtle, projects 3-5 feet, requires multiple spritzes

**Longevity:**
- Oud: Lasts 10+ hours, sometimes all day
- French: Typically 4-6 hours

**Opening:**
- Oud: Hits immediately—woody, deep, intense
- French: Subtle opening (often citrus), builds gradually

**Wearing occasions:**
- Oud: Evening wear, special occasions, intimate settings
- French: Daily wear, office, any occasion

**Price:**
- Oud: Premium (reflects rarity and luxury)
- French: Mid-to-premium

Which is "better"? Neither. They're designed for different purposes and audiences.

[SECTION 4: WHICH SHOULD YOU USE? - 4:30-4:50]
If you're starting a fragrance brand:
- Oud is great if your target customer values luxury, tradition, and unique scents
- French is great if you want broad appeal, elegance, and wearability

At Scent Bazaar, we carry both—premium ouds from traditional suppliers and French-style
fragrance concentrates from European houses. We also have synthetic oud for brands
that want oud's character at a more accessible price point.

[OUTRO - 4:50-5:00]

either option for your fragrance project, we've got both.
```

```
Bakhoor is a blend of fragrant wood chips, essential oils, and resins—usually oud,
sandalwood, or rose—burned in a special incense burner.

[SHOW BAKHOOR PRODUCT]

[HOW TO BURN IT - 0:45-1:30]

Step 2: Place your bakhoor on a piece of aluminum foil inside the burner.

rise up.

Step 4: As the bakhoor heats, it releases fragrance gradually. The scent will fill
a room (or entire house) in minutes.

[DEMO BURNING BAKHOOR]

[TIPS - 1:30-1:50]

- Keep your burner away from fabrics and children
```
